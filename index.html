Team

All owners of the 116 homes of Greenfield Estates subdivision are members of the Homeowners Association more commonly referred to as the HOA.


The HOA board of directors are homeowners of the community and these Directors are not paid for the work that they do for the community and receive no other benefits.


The primary objective of the HOA Board is to work in partnership with all Greenfield Estates Owners (Association members) and contractors to make our subdivision one of the most attractive communities in the Aynor area in which you are proud to call Greenfield Estates Home.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • Is there an HOA Law in South Carolina?

    Yes, It was signed by the Governor on May 17, 2018 and is referred to as the SC Bill 3886.

    The two links below refer to the actual law, and a summary about how the law will impact a homeowner.

    Copy of South Carolina's HOA Law

  • Who should I contact if I spot a burned out street light?

    Notify the HOA's email contact at GreenfieldEstatesHOA@gmail.com or directly report the problem to Horry Electric at the following link:http://www.horryelectric.com/report-a-streetsecurity-light-out/

    Each light has a number on the pole which would need to be communicated to the electric company for the light to be replaced.

  • When are assessments due and how should I pay?

    Assessments are due Annually, and will be mailed out by January 15th.

    Payment can be mailed or paid online utilizing the portal.

    Assessment Dues are payable by March 15th of every year, and a late fee will be assessed to the account on March 16th if dues are not received.

  • Since the developer and builders are finished, why don't we just dissolve the HOA?

    This may be a valid option for some communities; especially very small ones with no amenities. However, there are several reasons we should not dissolve our HOA.

    Our HOA has expenses that the HOA Annual Assessments are utilized to help cover these expenses.

    The HOA owns & maintains three ponds, has threewetlands, two common areas, private roads, maintains 18 street lights, liability insurance as well as D&O insurance coverage, and landscaping of four brick entry signs.

    The HOA enhances the value of our homes.

  • Why is architectural approval necessary prior to making changes to my property?

    Architectural approval is essential to ensuring that all visible changes are in keeping with the community’s standards for construction and are aesthetically pleasing to the community. Continued adherence with these standards ensures long-term enjoyment of the community, protected curb appeal and property values.

  • How important is a Reserve Account, and how do we determine an appropriate amount needed in this fund?

    Properly funding a Reserve Account is essential to the association’s fiscal responsibility. Preparing for long-term expenses associated with the maintenance and improvement of association-owned amenities will prevent unexpected Special Assessments, which typically require association member approval. Long-range budget studies include consideration of the life expectancy of property owned by the HOA: Roads, Commons Areas, Entry signs, and landscape components associated with the property.

  • How can I obtain a copy of my restrictive covenants(CC&R) or Bylaws? What are Bylaws?

    You should have received a copy of your association’s restrictive covenants and Bilaws at closing. If you did not, a copy is available to view and print on the website, or e-mail GreenfieldestatesHOA@gmail.com and we will e-mail it to you.

    The Bylaws serve as an organizational manual and will help guide the HOA through the orderly business process and operation rules. They establish procedures for the HOA in regards to holding elections, organizing meetings, quorum requirements, membership structure (if needed) and other essential operations of the nonprofit.

  • Why is it important to attend my association’s annual meetings?

    Members of the association not only have the right to vote on association business matters, but also have the responsibility to ensure that quorum requirements are met for transacting association business. The quorum requirements outlined in the Association’s By-Laws are intended to ensure appropriate owner representation to conduct business affecting all members of the association.

  • Who handles animal control issues?

    If a stray shows up, you can take it to the Animal Care Center, Monday through Friday from 11:00 AM to 5:00 PM, or you can contact animal control at (843) 248-1520 to come and pick it up. Please note that they do not pick up stray cats unless they are already captured. If you cannot catch it, you will need to purchase, rent or borrow a trap from a friend or relative and capture the cat.

  • Who are members of the association?

    Every homeowner within the community is a member. When the community was built, the association was established with legal governing documents that made the community a deed restricted community; meaning, whoever holds a deed to land in the community must comply with the governing documents. When you purchased your home, your deed to your property obligates you to the association as a member.

    Membership is mandatory.

  • How can governing documents be changed?

    The Bylaws and CC&R's can be changed with 3/4 of the Total Association Vote. The approved Amendments must be drafted by an attorney, notarized and filed with the county. Unless significant changes are needed, it is generally best to leave the original CC&R's in place despite considerable verbiage about the "declarant", which is the builder/developer.

    The Rules and Regulations which includes Architectural Design and Review Guidelines, Community-Wide Standard Guidelines and Policies can be clarified, updated and in some cases changed as needed only with approval by the HOA board of directors but cannot contradict the CC&R's.
